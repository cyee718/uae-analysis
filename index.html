Overview of Trends and Policy Impact

From tourism to real estate…and so on.

UAE Real GDP

Besides from oil exports that the nation is renounced for, the nation did practical things that made the rapid recoveries from economic downturns.

In 2009 it kept credit moving and kept building, so companies could breathe from the Global Financial Crisis. In 2015, global oil prices fell significantly due to a surge in US shale oil production, coupled with OPEC's decision to maintain production levels despite the growing supply glut. This caused major economic slowdown in oil producing countries, such as the UAE and China.

To its immediate action, the UAE deregulated fuel prices and introduced Bankcruptcy Law, so firms could restructure rather than collapse. The nation also pushed harder into other non-oil trades such as logistics and services, made it easier to start a business, and expanded JAFZA free zones.

Of more significance is the UAE's rebound from COVID, which struck many nations with detrimental shocks. In 2020 to 2022 it reopened to tourists early with strong vaccination, and ran Expo 2020 to pull in demand. Immediately following, the UAE introduced the Golden and Green visas, which allowed stakeholders to retain legal residence in the UAE for longer. Opportunity-wise, the UAE also introduced remote work and retirement options, allowed full foreign ownership, and switched the workweek to Monday to Friday, which resulted in fewer cracks in the downturn and more momentum in the upswing.

Source: World Bank (real GDP).

GCC Nationals Visiting the UAE

The following visual shows the number of GCC nationals visiting the UAE from 2010 to 2015.

Between 2013 and 2015, the number of GCC nationals visiting the UAE rose sharply, with total regional arrivals increasing from under 1 million in 2013 to over 4 million by 2015- a near 300% surge. This spike coincided with several macroeconomic and policy developments.

In late 2013, Dubai secured its bid to host Expo 2020, triggering extensive infrastructure investment and a boost in regional tourism confidence. Real GDP growth in the UAE remained strong during this period, averaging 4.5% annually, supported by diversification efforts and high public spending despite global oil price fluctuations.

Additionally, the UAE’s political stability and liberalised retail and hospitality sectors positioned it as a safe, high-value destination amid broader regional uncertainty following the Arab Spring. The increase was especially notable among Saudi and Qatari visitors, likely driven by eased cross-border mobility, rising disposable incomes, and the appeal of Dubai’s growing status as a luxury and events tourism hub.
